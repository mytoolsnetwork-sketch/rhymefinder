What Rhymes With Love?

"Love" is one of the most searched rhymes in English — and one of the trickiest. It has very few perfect rhymes, which is why so many songwriters and poets rely on near rhymes and slant rhymes instead. Understanding all three categories gives you far more options to work with.

Perfect Rhymes for Love

The perfect rhymes for "love" are limited but powerful: above, dove, glove, and shove. "Above" is by far the most commonly used in songs and poetry — it's natural, unforced, and carries emotional weight. "Dove" works beautifully in romantic and spiritual contexts. "Glove" and "shove" are harder to use naturally but can work in the right context.

Near Rhymes for Love — Why Songwriters Love Them

Near rhymes (also called slant rhymes or half rhymes) share similar sounds without matching exactly. For "love," the most popular near rhymes in hit songs are move, groove, prove, soothe, and smooth. These work because the vowel sound is close enough that the ear accepts it as a rhyme, especially in a musical context where melody carries the connection.

Words like moon, tune, soon, and truth are also widely used — they create a dreamy, romantic sound that suits love songs particularly well.

Using Love in Poetry

In poetry, slant rhymes give you even more freedom. Words like enough, rough, tough, and flood all share consonant sounds with "love" and can create a raw, emotional texture that perfect rhymes sometimes lack. Some of the most powerful love poems use slant rhymes precisely because the slight mismatch creates tension — which mirrors the complexity of love itself.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a perfect rhyme for love?

The perfect rhymes for "love" are above, dove, glove, and shove. "Above" is the most versatile and commonly used in both songs and poetry.

What word rhymes with love in a song?

In songs, near rhymes are just as effective as perfect rhymes. "Move," "groove," "prove," and "soothe" are all popular choices in love songs because they flow naturally and carry a similar emotional tone.

Why is love hard to rhyme?

"Love" has an unusual vowel sound (the short "u" followed by "v") that doesn't appear in many common English words. This is why so many songwriters and poets use near rhymes or slant rhymes instead of forcing a perfect rhyme that sounds unnatural.

What rhymes with love and heart?

For a couplet using both "love" and "heart," try pairing "above" with "apart" or "start" — these create a satisfying near-rhyme scheme that's been used in countless songs and poems.
